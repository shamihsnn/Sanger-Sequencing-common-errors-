Reference Standard
What a Clean Chromatogram Looks Like
Each base position produces one dominant peak in its corresponding color channel. The other three channels remain near baseline. Peaks are sharp, evenly spaced, and uniformly tall.
A — Adenine C — Cytosine G — Guanine T — Thymine

🔍 How to Inspect a Trace

  • Look for one sharp peak per position with a flat baseline between peaks.
  • The middle of the read (bases ~50–600) is the most reliable region.
  • The first 20–40 bases are often noisy due to unincorporated primers.
  • Signal quality drops toward the end as reagents are depleted.
  • Always compare forward and reverse reads before calling a variant.

⚠️ Reading Rule

If the trace is mixed from the very beginning, suspect contamination or multiple templates. If it becomes mixed only after a specific position, suspect a heterozygous indel or secondary priming site.

✅ Quality Indicators

Clean traces have a Phred quality score ≥ Q20 (99% accuracy) across the usable region. Signal-to-noise ratio should be 100–750. Peak heights should be relatively uniform without abrupt changes.

Heterozygous SNP vs. Mixed Template

Het SNP: Double peak at one specific position; rest of trace is clean. Peak ratio is ~1:1, consistent in both forward and reverse reads.

Mixed template: Double peaks throughout the entire read. Peak ratios vary, and the pattern often differs between forward and reverse reads.

Homozygous Variant vs. Clean Wild-Type

A homozygous variant looks identical to a clean read by visual inspection alone — single sharp peaks with no overlap. The only clue is a mismatch when aligned to the reference sequence. This is why reference comparison is mandatory.

Indel vs. Contamination

Indel: Clean start, then a sudden permanent phase shift at the indel site. Everything downstream shows overlapping double peaks.

Contamination: The mess starts from the very beginning of the trace, not at a specific transition point.

Quality Metrics
Phred Quality Scores & What They Mean
Phred scores quantify base-call confidence: Q = −10 × log₁₀(P), where P is the error probability.
Q10
90% Accuracy

1 in 10 error. Unreliable — trim or discard these bases.

Q20
99% Accuracy

1 in 100 error. Minimum acceptable quality for most applications.

Q30
99.9% Accuracy

1 in 1,000 error. High-quality; benchmark for sequencing standards.

Q40
99.99% Accuracy

1 in 10,000 error. Excellent — rarely achieved at read ends.

Best Practices
Template Preparation & Primer Design

🧪 Template Quality

  • Plasmid DNA: 150–300 ng per reaction
  • PCR products: 10–40 ng per reaction
  • A260/A280: 1.8–2.0 (protein-free)
  • A260/A230: ≥ 2.0 (salt/organic-free)
  • Use fluorometric quantification (Qubit) over NanoDrop for accuracy
  • Avoid EDTA, excess salts, and residual ethanol

🎯 Primer Design

  • Length: 18–24 nucleotides
  • GC content: 40–60%
  • Melting temperature (Tₘ): 50–65 °C
  • Prime ≥ 100 bp away from the region of interest
  • Avoid secondary structure, primer dimers, and homopolymeric runs
  • One primer per reaction — do not mix forward and reverse

✅ Validation Checklist

  • Always sequence bidirectionally (forward + reverse)
  • Align to a reference sequence
  • Trim bases with Q < 20
  • Inspect raw .ab1 files in a trace viewer (e.g., SnapGene, Sequence Scanner)
  • Check that PCR yields a single clean band on gel
  • For critical variants, repeat the sequencing from fresh template
Practical Cautions
What Not to Over-Claim

🚫 Do Not Trust the Tail

End-of-read quality decline is expected. Base calls from the far end (typically past base ~700) are less reliable. Always trim before interpreting.

🚫 Do Not Rely on One Direction

A single forward trace can mislead about homozygous changes, dye artifacts, and local noise. The reverse read is essential for confirmation.

🚫 Do Not Confuse Saturation with Biology

Overloaded signal creates spectral pull-up — ghost peaks in other channels that mimic heterozygosity. Dilute and re-run before calling a variant.
